# Content Pillars — Shell Trade

> Five recurring content categories. Every piece of content the Content Director assigns should map to exactly one pillar (occasionally two). If it doesn't map to any pillar, it doesn't get made.

## 1. Trading Education

**Purpose:** Teach real, transferable trading concepts using the game's own curriculum and boss fights as the vehicle. This pillar is the credibility engine — it's what separates Shell Trade from hype accounts and is the top-of-funnel content that eventually feeds the paid course.

**Examples:**
- "Why your stop-loss saved you $400" — a `risk_management_success` event turned into a 30-second breakdown.
- "The difference between a breakout and a fakeout" — sourced from a `boss_defeated` event on Boss 1, The Fakeout.
- "What is VWAP and why do institutions care" — sourced from Boss 4, The VWAP Phantom, lesson content.
- "Reading a candle in 10 seconds" — Boss 0, The Market Maker, distilled into a quick-hit explainer.

**Platform adaptation:** TikTok/Shorts/Reels get the 15–30 second hook-and-payoff version with on-screen text overlay; YouTube long-form gets a fuller breakdown (3–8 minutes) stitched from multiple related events; X gets a thread version with screenshots/charts as the visual; Instagram gets a carousel version of the same thread for static-feed audiences.

## 2. Turtle Journey

**Purpose:** Serialized, character-driven progression content — Shell's own leveling-up arc, told episodically. This pillar builds parasocial attachment to the mascot the same way a long-running web series builds attachment to its lead, and it's the pillar most likely to be watched/followed for its own sake rather than for the lesson.

**Examples:**
- "Shell just ranked up from Crab 🦀 to Pufferfish 🐡 — here's what changed" — sourced from a `level_up` event.
- "Shell vs. The Liquidator: the boss that almost ended the run" — sourced from a `boss_encounter` + `boss_defeated` pair, told as a mini-narrative.
- "Shell's first trade ever (it did not go well)" — early-curriculum nostalgia content once enough history exists.
- Weekly "Shell's Week" recap stitching together the best events from the last seven days into one episodic update.

**Platform adaptation:** YouTube is the primary home for serialized "episodes"; TikTok/Shorts get cold-open teaser cuts of the same footage; X gets a text recap with a key clip attached; Instagram Reels mirrors the TikTok cut with platform-native captioning.

## 3. Build In Public

**Purpose:** Show the actual construction of the game and the content engine itself — feature ships, architecture decisions, growth numbers, setbacks. This pillar recruits the builder/indie-dev audience, builds trust through transparency, and recursively markets the very pipeline that produces all the other pillars.

**Examples:**
- "We just shipped Boss 3: The Structure Shark — here's the design process."
- "Our content pipeline posted its first fully AI-assisted clip today — here's how it works."
- Weekly/monthly metrics post: players, retention, content output volume.
- "Here's a bug that took us three days to find" — credibility through honesty about setbacks.

**Platform adaptation:** X is the primary home (the build-in-public audience over-indexes there); long-form versions go to YouTube as dev-log style videos; TikTok/Reels get a condensed visual cut when there's something genuinely visual to show (UI, gameplay, a chart); Instagram is lower priority for this pillar unless there's a strong visual asset.

## 4. Trading Memes

**Purpose:** Pure culture/humor content using the deadpan-turtle-versus-chaotic-market contrast (see `brand-voice.md`). This pillar is the top-of-funnel reach driver — lowest barrier to share, broadest possible audience, and the on-ramp for people who would never click an "educational" headline.

**Examples:**
- Shell, unbothered, sitting through a red candle crash while everything around it is on fire.
- "POV: you didn't set a stop-loss" — reaction-format meme using a `trade_loss` event as the punchline setup.
- Format jokes built on rank names (e.g., "things only a Plankton 🦠 would do" vs. "things a Whale 🐋 would never do").
- Relatable-chaos formats borrowed from broader meme culture, re-skinned with Shell Trade's turtle and chart visuals.

**Platform adaptation:** TikTok and X are the primary homes (fastest meme velocity); Instagram gets the static-image meme format; Reels mirrors TikTok; YouTube is not a meme-pillar platform and should rarely if ever carry this content as a standalone upload (it can live inside a Shorts feed instead).

## 5. Market Education

**Purpose:** Broader market/crypto literacy that isn't tied to a specific in-game event — explaining the assets themselves (BTC, ETH, SOL, XRP, DOGE, BNB), market structure concepts, or current market context. This pillar widens the funnel beyond "people already in the game" to general crypto-curious audiences searching for context.

**Examples:**
- "Why does Bitcoin have a 21 million coin cap and why does that matter" — sourced from the in-game BTC faction description.
- "Dogecoin has no supply cap — here's what that means for volatility" — sourced from the DOGE faction's risk profile.
- "What 'institutional' actually means when people say XRP is built for banks."
- Light, non-promissory market-context explainers timed to genuinely newsworthy crypto moments (never paired with price predictions or calls).

**Platform adaptation:** YouTube and X favor the longer explainer format; TikTok/Shorts/Reels get a tight 30–45 second version with strong visual hooks (faction art, live chart visuals); Instagram gets a carousel breaking the explainer into swipeable slides.
